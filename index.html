Deep Learning

Interactive AI Learning Studio

An academia-first platform for intuitive, visual, and discussion-driven learning across MLPs, optimizers, CNNs, sequence models, and retrieval-augmented generation.

Why now

The gap in AI pedagogy

Theory is abstract

Students often memorize layers, optimizers, and equations without seeing how architecture choices alter behavior.

Coding hides intuition

Students spend time on syntax and debugging before understanding when to use dropout, batch normalization, residual paths, or chunking strategies.

Discussion lacks evidence

Classroom comparison becomes stronger when students defend optimizer choices, dataset setup, and model variants using visual results.

Lab 1

MLP Studio. Architecture before code.

Students begin with a binary or multiclass dataset, then toggle hidden layers, neurons, activations, dropout, batch normalization, optimizer, learning rate, batch size, and epochs.

The interface helps learners see how capacity, regularization, and optimization affect convergence, accuracy, generalization, and representation quality.

Every change is linked to a visible consequence so students can defend the architecture they chose.

MLP evidence

What students see and reason about

Performance traces. Train and validation accuracy and loss reveal underfitting, overfitting, and unstable training dynamics.
Representation plots. Feature views after dimensionality reduction help students connect learned spaces to class separability.
Regularization effects. Dropout and batch normalization become visible learning mechanisms rather than textbook definitions.
Design lesson. The model is not just trained; it is interpreted as a system.
Lab 2

Optimizer Playground. Make geometry intuitive.

Students compare GD, SGD, momentum, RMSProp, Adam, and related methods on convex and non-convex objective surfaces.

The visual focus is not only speed, but also trajectory. Local minima, global minima, maxima, plateaus, and saddle regions become visible.

The point of the lab is to explain why different update rules behave differently under curvature, noise, and narrow valleys.

Optimization outcomes

From formula memorization to experimental comparison

Surfaces to test

Convex bowls, non-convex multi-peak surfaces, saddle points, and narrow ravines show why optimization is not one-size-fits-all.

Discussion prompt

Which optimizer is robust, which is fast, and which one fails on sharp or deceptive landscapes?

Algorithm lens

GD is steady, SGD is noisy, momentum accelerates consistent direction, RMSProp adapts step scale, and Adam blends the useful parts of both families.

Lab 3

CNN Vision Lab. From kernels to feature maps.

Students build convolutional networks with editable kernels, padding, stride, pooling, dense layers, and dropout.

Template pathways can expose canonical variants such as simple CNN, deep CNN, VGG-style, ResNet-style, or mobile-efficient designs.

Feature maps make it clear how filters move from edges and strokes to more abstract structures across depth.

Lab 4

Sequence Lab. RNN, LSTM, and GRU side by side.

Students select sequence tasks such as fraud detection, load forecasting, sentiment analysis, or entity recognition, then compare recurrent variants directly.

Hidden size, sequence length, learning rate, epochs, and gate ablations connect architecture design with task demands.

The side-by-side view supports explanation of vanishing gradients, gating, memory retention, and computational trade-offs.

Lab 5

RAG Lab. Retrieval made visible end to end.

Learners preprocess PDFs, chunk content, embed chunks with different models, and query an open-source LLM.

The system contrasts plain LLM responses with RAG-grounded responses so students can judge factuality, relevance, and source traceability.

This makes hallucination, grounding, and retrieval quality visible rather than theoretical.

RAG embeddings

Chunking and embedding as geometry

Students inspect how chunking strategy changes the embedding cloud, neighborhood structure, and retrieval behavior.

This makes vector search and semantic grouping easier to explain in a classroom setting.

The geometry of chunks becomes the explanation for why some answers surface and others do not.

Pedagogy design

Each lab connects exploration, discussion, and assignment

Explore visually
Students change architecture and hyperparameters without touching implementation details at first exposure.
Discuss choices
Peers compare settings, justify trade-offs, and explain why a configuration helped or failed on a dataset.
Transfer to practice
Assignments require applying the same ideas to a new dataset or use case selected by the learner.
Closing

Teaching AI models as systems students can see, compare, and defend

This platform positions AI education as interactive scientific reasoning. Students build intuition first, validate experimentally, discuss evidence, and transfer learning to authentic problems.

A reusable visual lab ecosystem can support lectures, guided labs, project-based courses, and education research.
